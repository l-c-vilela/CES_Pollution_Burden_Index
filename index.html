The Problem: Addressing "Missing Vulnerability"

Standard federal models like EJScreen are often limited to measuring "what is in the air" while ignoring "who breathes it." This approach neglects the underlying health fragility and economic barriers that define true environmental risk.

On March 13, 2026, a federal judge in D.C. dismissed a lawsuit to force the government to bring the CEJST tool back online, making independent audits a critical necessity for community protection.

The EJ Baseline: Historical Frequency Audit

The baseline for this audit is established through a 10-year historical tally (2015–2024). For every census tract in the United States, we calculated how many times it was officially designated as a federal priority area over the last decade.

The Visibility Index

The raw frequency count (0–10) is converted into a national percentile score (0–100). This indicates a tract's relative visibility to federal policy:

  • Score of 0: The tract has been invisible to federal priority filters for a decade.
  • Score of 100: The tract has been a constant, high-visibility priority for 10 years.

The Interaction Engine

We utilize the CalEnviroScreen (CES) framework from the OEHHA. This model identifies communities burdened by the intersection of high pollution and high population vulnerability using a multiplier logic.

The Cumulative Impact Formula
Score = Pollution × Vulnerability

By multiplying factors, we ensure that high pollution in a fragile community (e.g., high asthma or heart disease rates) results in an exponentially higher risk score than in a healthy one.

Why California?

  • Workforce: Maintains over 545,000 clean energy jobs as of 2025.
  • Legal Mandate: CES is the legal requirement for allocating Cap-and-Trade and state grant revenue in California.
